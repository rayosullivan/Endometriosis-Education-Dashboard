Endometriosis Education Platform with Clinician Dashboard and RAG Patient Assistant
Executive summary
Prof.Ray, an endometriosis information and education platform that includes a clinician-facing dashboard plus a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) patient assistant should be designed as a regulated-health, safety-critical information system, even if the initial product positioning is “education” rather than “diagnosis.” This is because the moment the assistant performs symptom triage, risk scoring, or escalation, it begins to influence health decisions and therefore triggers higher expectations for clinical safety engineering, auditability, and human oversight. Under the EU AI Act, AI systems used for emergency healthcare patient triage are explicitly highlighted as high-risk in critical, life-and-health situations, and high-risk system requirements emphasise risk management, logging, transparency, human oversight, and robustness. 

A robust reference architecture has three separations that reduce both risk and complexity:

Content governance plane (clinician dashboard): authoring, upload, metadata, structured review, versioning, and publication control—aligned to guideline-based content such as the Endometriosis: diagnosis and management (NG73) and the ESHRE Guideline: Endometriosis (2022), plus Irish pathways in A National Framework for the Management of Endometriosis in Ireland. 

Retrieval plane (RAG ingestion + indexing): a secure ingestion pipeline for URLs/PDFs/Word documents, metadata extraction, chunking, embeddings, vector indexing, and refresh policies; designed to constrain generation to retrieved sources and to return citation-backed answers—a design direction consistent with the original RAG framing that retrieval helps with provenance and updating knowledge. 

Safety and compliance plane (cross-cutting): GDPR-by-design data minimisation, consent capture, data subject rights handling, DPIA, and security controls; plus AI Act-aligned logging, human oversight, user transparency (“you are interacting with an AI system”), and staff AI literacy. 

The most defensible “MVP” profile is to store only first name + email (as requested) and keep all symptom narrative and chat content ephemeral by default, with explicit opt-in if patients want to save a symptom diary or generate a GP note. This maps cleanly to GDPR principles of purpose limitation and data minimisation, while still allowing the assistant to deliver 24/7 Q&A and structured questionnaires. 

Clinical and product requirements
Endometriosis is a chronic condition with wide-ranging symptom burden and quality-of-life impact; authoritative sources emphasise pain, bleeding and fertility impacts, and the need for timely access to care. 
 Diagnostic delay remains a persistent problem in the literature, supporting product features that help women organise symptoms, recognise patterns, and communicate effectively with primary care. 

From a product-scoping standpoint, the safest framing is:

Patient assistant: education, navigation of care pathways, symptom journaling, questionnaire delivery, and preparation of a GP summary—while avoiding diagnostic claims and clearly routing emergencies to urgent services. This aligns with guideline advice to use symptom diaries and to suspect endometriosis with specific symptom clusters (cyclical pelvic, bowel, urinary symptoms, dyspareunia, infertility association). 
Clinician dashboard: a controlled publishing pipeline for content bundles (guidelines, local Irish pathways, clinic-specific leaflets), with traceable sign-off and versioning—so the assistant’s retrieval corpus stays clinically curated and defensible. 
Ireland pathway awareness: the Irish national framework describes a structured continuum of care beginning in primary care and defines access criteria across care levels; incorporating this into both retrieval and the GP note output makes the platform immediately more locally actionable. 
A key regulatory nuance: if the platform’s “intended purpose” shifts toward clinical decision support (e.g., “this score indicates likely endometriosis” or “triage category”), EU medical device software guidance treats the manufacturer’s intended purpose and promotional claims as central to qualification and classification, and explicitly notes that software guidance applies to apps operating on mobile or cloud platforms.

This architecture supports two primary workflows:

Clinician content workflow (governed ingestion): upload or register a URL → malware/SSRF-safe fetch → text extraction (PDF/DOCX/HTML) → metadata extraction → chunking → embeddings → vector index update → publication of a “corpus version” for the assistant. Secure, versioned content pipelines are a practical way to meet transparency and traceability expectations for higher-risk uses. 

Patient Q&A workflow (RAG with safety gating): user query → red-flag and intent detection → retrieval (top-k) → answer synthesis constrained to the retrieved context → citations returned (document title + section + date) → if threshold triggers fire, provide escalation instructions and optionally notify clinicians. RAG-style systems were proposed to improve factuality and provenance by grounding generation on an external knowledge index. 

RAG ingestion of URLs/PDF/Word
A defensible ingestion pipeline should implement:

Acquisition controls: allow-list domains for URL ingestion; block link-local and private IP ranges to prevent SSRF; enforce timeouts and content-length limits; and store the fetched artifact immutably with hash and timestamp for provenance. SSRF and insecure design are mainstream web risks; prompt-injection and data exfiltration are now prominent GenAI app risks. 
Malware scanning: scan every uploaded PDF/DOCX and fetched payload before parsing; quarantine on detection; log outcomes. (In regulated health contexts, this is “table stakes” rather than optional.) 
Parsing and normalisation: convert to a canonical text+structure representation (headings, tables, lists), preserving page numbers and section anchors for citations.
Metadata extraction: authoring organisation, publication date, jurisdiction (Ireland/UK/EU), document type (guideline, patient leaflet), evidence strength tags, and “expiry/review date.”
Chunking: semantic chunking aligned to headings; with overlap; chunk IDs stable across re-ingestions where possible to reduce embedding churn.
Indexing: store chunks in a vector store plus a sparse index (BM25) for hybrid retrieval where needed, especially for acronyms and medication names.
Refresh policies: scheduled recrawl for URLs, and clinician-triggered “reindex now,” with a corpus version bump and rollback capability.
Model orchestration, logging and auditability
Given the EU AI Act’s emphasis on record-keeping/logs for high-risk systems and lifecycle risk management, the platform should treat model logs as a first-class system of record—capturing prompts, retrieved sources, model versions, safety-rule outcomes, and user-visible outputs in a controlled way. 
 In practice, this means:

Orchestrator-managed prompts (not user-composed), and a strict separation between user message, retrieved context, and system instructions.
Tamper-evident logging: append-only logs with access control and integrity protection; retention aligned to purpose (e.g., safety incident investigation). High-risk systems must support traceability through logging. 
Human oversight hooks: clinician review queues for flagged interactions and a mechanism to “stop” or override automated behaviour, aligning with the AI Act’s requirement to enable human oversight and mitigate automation bias. 
Clinician dashboard and patient assistant UX
Clinician dashboard capabilities
A clinician dashboard is the operational control point for both clinical correctness and medico-legal defensibility. The following features are high-leverage:

Content upload and provenance

Upload PDF/DOCX, paste text, or register a URL with allow-list enforcement.
Auto-extract: title, headings, page markers, references, publication date, and jurisdiction tags.
Provenance fields: “source type” (guideline, local leaflet), “owner,” “import method,” and SHA-256 hash.
Metadata, versioning and lifecycle

Content item versioning (v1, v2…) and corpus release versioning (corpus-2026-02-01).
“Effective date” and “review-by date” to enforce refresh (NICE updates and local frameworks change; HSE pages also have review dates). 
Rollback to prior corpus on detected errors.
Review workflows

Draft → clinical review → sign-off → publish, with role-based signatories and required two-person rule for certain content classes (e.g., triage thresholds). This echoes safety culture expectations even where not formally mandated. 
“Change request” mechanism and paper-trail: who changed what, why, and which evidence supported it.
Access controls

Role-based access control (RBAC): author, reviewer, publisher, auditor, admin. OWASP emphasises broken access control as a leading web risk and RBAC is a standard mitigation. 
Tenant isolation if multiple clinics/organisations are served.
Patient assistant UX requirements
Tone and interaction model

The assistant should default to: brief empathy, clarity, non-judgment, and a consistent pattern: reflect → answer with citations → suggest next step → safety check. Endometriosis guidance emphasises the condition’s long-term physical and psychosocial impacts and the need to tailor information to the individual’s circumstances and priorities. 

Transparency and expectations

Up-front: “You are interacting with an AI system,” consistent with AI Act transparency obligations for AI systems intended to interact directly with natural persons. 
Always-visible: “Not a substitute for a clinician; emergencies go to 112/999.”
Red-flag recognition and escalation

A practical approach is a two-stage safety layer:

Rule-based “hard stops” (deterministic triggers): pregnancy + abdominal/pelvic pain and/or bleeding; fainting/collapse; sudden severe abdominal pain; vomiting blood; inability to pass urine or stool/gas; severe pain with peritonism cues; sepsis cues; etc. Irish public guidance for severe abdominal pain includes ED attendance for sudden/severe onset, blood in vomit/stool, inability to pass urine or stool/gas, and breathing/chest pain emergencies. 
 For early pregnancy, NICE explicitly lists ectopic pregnancy symptom patterns including abdominal/pelvic pain, missed period, bleeding, and syncope, supporting conservative escalation when pregnancy is possible. 

Model-assisted “soft flags” (probabilistic): escalating pain trajectories, bowel obstruction warning patterns, urinary obstruction narratives, or severe mental distress; these route to additional questions and may create a clinician alert depending on the user’s consent settings.

Escalation workflow design

If hard-stop: show emergency instruction, suppress routine educational output, and (if consented) trigger a clinician notification with the user’s minimal details and the risk trigger.
If soft-flag: ask 3–5 clarifying questions, then either (a) advise urgent care, or (b) proceed with educational content plus GP note generation.
Under the AI Act, systems must be designed so that humans can monitor, interpret, and override outputs and avoid automation bias—so escalation must be auditable and clinician-overridable. 

Questionnaire delivery, scoring, GP note generation

Questionnaire items should reflect guidelines and validated measures where possible. NICE recommends symptom diaries to support discussion. 
For endometriosis-specific quality-of-life measurement, the Endometriosis Health Profile instruments (EHP-30/EHP-5) are widely discussed in the literature but may have licensing requirements; if used, the platform should store the user’s permissions and the exact instrument version used. 
Scoring should be deterministic where possible (to reduce variability and medico-legal ambiguity), with the LLM used for interpretation and summarisation—not for arithmetic.

Governance, compliance, security, and clinical safety
GDPR design choices
The platform will process personal data, and if symptom or questionnaire data is stored it becomes “special category” health data requiring additional protections and a valid Article 9 condition; Irish regulator guidance highlights health data as special category data. 

A GDPR-by-design posture for this platform typically includes:

Lawful basis (Article 6): commonly contract (to provide the service) and/or consent (for optional saved chat, symptom diary, sharing with clinicians, research). Consent must be freely given, specific, informed and withdrawable; EDPB guidance provides detailed expectations around valid consent. 
Special category condition (Article 9): for stored symptom and treatment data, rely on explicit consent unless the system is deployed under a clinical care arrangement where another condition may apply (implementation-specific). 
Data minimisation & purpose limitation: store only what is needed, keep optional health data off by default, and avoid using patient content to improve models unless explicitly opted in. These principles are emphasised in EU guidance on GDPR principles. 
Data subject rights operationalisation: implement workflows for access and erasure requests; the Irish regulator provides practical descriptions of rights such as access and erasure. 
DPIA: given health data and novel technology, a DPIA is strongly indicated; the EDPB maintains DPIA materials and Irish regulator materials list processing types requiring DPIAs in practice. 
Retention & deletion: configure retention by data class (e.g., chat logs 0 days by default; saved GP notes 12 months unless user deletes; audit logs longer if needed for safety incident investigation). This must be stated clearly at consent capture. 
Pseudonymisation/anonymisation: separate identifiers (user_id) from clinical records (patient_id), and use keyed hashes for analytics where possible.
EU AI Act implications
If the assistant is used for symptom triage or escalation in a way that affects critical health decisions, the system design should assume it will be scrutinised under the AI Act’s high-risk framing for emergency healthcare patient triage systems. 
 Even where the initial product position is “education,” your engineering controls can be aligned early to the Act’s core high-risk requirements:

Risk management system: continuous lifecycle risk management, testing against predefined metrics and thresholds, and post-market monitoring inputs. 
Data governance: ensure training/validation/testing datasets (if you train) are relevant, representative, and bias-managed; even if you do not train models, this logic translates to safe corpus governance and evaluation datasets. 
Record-keeping/logging: automatic logs over the system lifetime to support traceability. 
Transparency: provide clear instructions and performance limitations to deployers, and user-facing transparency that they are interacting with an AI system. 
Human oversight: design so natural persons can monitor, interpret, override, and stop the system, with explicit attention to automation bias. 
Accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity: consistent performance and resilience to attacks (including dataset poisoning and adversarial behaviours). 
AI literacy: ensure staff using/operating the system have sufficient AI literacy. 
The Irish Government’s timeline notes that rules on Annex III high-risk use cases apply from August 2026, so building compliance workstreams into the roadmap is prudent. 

Security controls
A healthcare information platform should apply layered security controls spanning application, data, and AI-specific threats:

Encryption: TLS in transit; encryption at rest for DB/object store; and key management with separation of duties. 
Access control: RBAC, MFA for clinicians, least privilege service accounts, and tenant isolation. OWASP highlights broken access control as a primary web risk. 
Audit logs: append-only, protected audit trails for content changes, consent updates, clinician access, and AI interactions—supporting traceability expectations. 
Secure ingestion: SSRF prevention for URL ingestion, malware scanning, and content-type validation. 
GenAI threat controls: prompt injection defences, retrieval allow-lists, output filtering for unsafe advice, and protections against sensitive information disclosure—aligned with OWASP’s LLM risk framing. 
Clinical safety and medico-legal disclaimers
A clinical safety case for the assistant needs three artefacts:

Red-flag definitions: encoded determinants (pregnancy possible + pain/bleeding; sudden severe abdominal pain; fainting; blood in vomit/stool; inability to pass urine or stool/gas). Irish public guidance supports ED attendance for many of these severe abdominal scenarios. 
Triage thresholds and escalation rules: deterministic rules plus limited model-assisted interpretation; all escalations must be explainable (“triggered because: X+Y”).
Escalation workflows and documentation: user-facing emergency guidance; clinician notification only with explicit consent and clear response expectations (e.g., “this is not monitored in real time unless explicitly stated”).
The assistant should present a concise disclaimer at every encounter: it provides educational information; it cannot diagnose; and urgent symptoms require urgent services. For UK/Irish pregnancy-related severe pain/bleeding, NHS/HSE messaging supports contacting maternity units and calling emergency services for severe symptoms, reinforcing conservative escalation logic. 

RAG specifics, option comparisons, and roadmap
RAG retrieval design and hallucination mitigation
Recommended retrieval stack (increasing safety and answer quality):

Hybrid retrieval (sparse + dense): improve recall for clinical terms and acronyms.
Reranking (cross-encoder or quality reranker): reduce “semantic near misses.”
Citation enforcement: every clinical claim in the answer must have at least one retrieved citation; if retrieval confidence is low, the assistant must say it cannot answer from the approved corpus and suggest next steps.
Answer templates: demote free-form synthesis; prefer structured outputs (what it is, why it happens, options, when to seek care).
Corpus version pinning: each answer records which corpus version was used, supporting traceability and post-incident review. Logs and event recording are central to high-risk expectations.
